Halix Solutions is an AI-powered B2B SaaS for digital marketing agencies. As technical co-founder I took it from nothing to a live, paying product — and I was the sole technical owner, with no separate engineering team behind me. Here's what that actually involves, beyond the line on a résumé.

The full breakdown lives in [the case study](/work). This is the shorter version of what I learned holding all of it at once.

## Two surfaces, one design system

The product lives in two places that pull in opposite directions: a public marketing site that has to *sell*, and an authenticated, multi-tenant workspace that has to *work*. I owned the UI/UX for both — flows, wireframes, the visual system, and a component library shared across them.

The discipline was refusing to let them drift into two products. One token set, one component vocabulary, so a button on the landing page and a button inside the app read as the same company by the same hand. Multi-tenant also meant designing for *accounts*, not just users — clean separation of who sees what, enforced underneath by Row-Level Security rather than trusted to the UI to hide.

## A research pipeline with no research team

The core feature is automated market research: competitor intent-gap analysis and trend reports, for agencies who'd otherwise do this by hand. Under the interface it's a multi-stage LLM pipeline, with long-running analysis orchestrated as background jobs so a single slow run never blocks a request.

The hard part was never calling a model once. It was staging the work — designing the prompts and the end-to-end flow so raw signals move through each stage and come out as something an agency can hand a client. Reliability, not cleverness, was the whole game.

## Billing and security, early on purpose

Money and safety are easy to skip in a 0-to-1 and expensive to retrofit, so I wired both in before they were urgent. Billing runs on Stripe — subscription tiers plus usage-based pricing, kept in sync through webhooks. Security got real attention: Row-Level Security policies, rate limiting, OWASP-style audits, and secure-by-default permissions, so the database refuses bad access instead of trusting the app to ask nicely. It all ships through CI/CD that runs test and security gates and applies migrations before anything reaches production.

None of this is glamorous. All of it is the difference between a demo and a product people pay for.

## Shaping the story, not just the software

A technical co-founder who only writes code leaves the product half-sold. I also shaped brand and go-to-market — the landing narrative, the positioning, the marketing assets. And that fed straight back into the design: the same read on who the agency buyer is drove both the pitch on the marketing site and the flows inside the app.

That's the throughline. One person holding the product, the engineering direction, and the story it tells — together — and carrying it from zero to a live, paying SaaS.

## What it taught me

Wearing every hat forces a kind of honesty. There's no one to hand the unglamorous decision to, and no brief to hide behind when a call comes out wrong. You learn to make pragmatic trade-offs under real constraints, and you learn which corners are load-bearing and which ones you can leave rough for now. Mostly you learn that shipping is a series of those judgments, made quickly, owned completely.
