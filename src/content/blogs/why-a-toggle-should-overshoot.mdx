A toggle that slides linearly from off to on works. It's also dead — the kind of motion you'd never describe as feeling like anything. Here's the one I actually ship. Flip it a few times.

The difference between this and a CSS `transition: left 200ms` is small in code and large in the hand. It comes down to three deliberate choices, none of which a user would consciously notice — which is exactly the point.

## The thumb overshoots, then settles

The thumb doesn't ease into the far edge. It rides a `back.out(2.4)` spring, so it travels *past* its resting position and rebounds back into place. That tiny overshoot-and-settle is what sells the "click." Real switches have momentum; they snap and bounce against their stop. A linear tween throws all of that away and you feel the absence even if you can't name it.

`back.out(2.4)` is a specific number, not a default. Lower and the overshoot disappears; higher and the thumb visibly springs like jelly. 2.4 is the largest bounce that still reads as a mechanism rather than a toy.

## It squashes along the direction of travel

While the thumb moves, it squashes — `scaleX` dips to about 86% and rounds back out at rest. The squash is anchored to the *leading* edge: `transform-origin` is `right center` on the way on, `left center` on the way back. So it compresses into the wall it's heading for, like a physical object meeting resistance, instead of shrinking symmetrically in place.

It's a 14% deformation that lasts 120 milliseconds. You won't see it if you look for it. You'll feel its absence if it's gone.

## The track changes color in lockstep

The track crossfades from neutral to accent across the same throw, in lockstep with the thumb — not before, not after. When two things that should be one motion drift out of sync by even a few frames, the whole interaction reads as cheap. Keeping them welded together is most of what "polish" means here.

## It's still a real switch

None of this costs accessibility, because the physics live on top of a real control. It's a `role="switch"` with `aria-checked`, focusable with Tab, flipped with Space or Enter, and it carries a visible focus ring. The animation is decoration over a correct, semantic foundation — never a `<div onClick>` pretending to be a control.

And for anyone who's asked their OS to minimize motion, the thumb just snaps to position with no overshoot and no squash. That's not a degraded fallback I tolerated; it's a path I designed. An animation that ignores `prefers-reduced-motion` is a bug, not a feature.

## The rule under all of it

Motion is feedback, not decoration. Every one of these choices answers a question the user is already asking — *did that register?* — and answers it the way a physical object would. That's the whole job. When motion stops answering a question, I cut it.
